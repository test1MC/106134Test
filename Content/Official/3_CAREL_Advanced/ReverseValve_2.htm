General operations

The 4-way reversing valve is servo-controlled valve used to reverse refrigerant flow in many air conditioning units or chillers. It allows the changeover from Heat pump to Cooling mode and vice versa. Figure 1 shows its three basic components: the solenoid coil, the pilot valve and the main valve body, with the slider.

Figure 1.

 

The valve slider is shifted by changes in differential pressure in the valve main body, which is actuated by the pilot solenoid valve. The valve slider is especially designed to prevent incomplete changeover and movement with minimum pressure differential.

Winter conditions

When the solenoid coil is energised, the pilot valve moves to the right, and the high pressure fluid enters into the piston chamber. On the other hand, the fluid is released from the piston chamber, and the piston and the slide valve portion moves to the right.

Figure 2.

Summer conditions

When the solenoid coil is de-energised, the pilot valve moves to the left, and the high pressure fluid comes into the discharge capillary tube, and enters into the piston chamber. On the other hand, the fluid is released from the piston chamber, and the piston and the slide valve portion moves to the left.

Figure 3.

Movement of the 4-way valve will be performed only if:

  • the pressure difference (DeltaP_RV) is above the threshold stated by the manufacturer (DeltaPThrsh_RV)
  • the unit has the possibility to reverse refrigerant flow (En_RV =TRUE).

If the difference in pressure is not sufficient (i.e. DeltaP_RV <DeltaPThrsh_RV), it will be necessary to wait until the minimum threshold is reached before controlling the movement of the valve. Figure 4 summarises operation.

Figure 4.

In case of blackouts, the solenoid that moves the valve is no longer energised and looses its position. The valve slider on the other hand can return to its normal position, remaining in step with the solenoid or can keep the last position before the blackout, losing the step with the solenoid, depending on the pressure status inside the main valve bodythe . Figure 5 illustrates the behaviour of the slider during a blackout:

  • before the blackout, (figure 5a and 5d) the solenoid valve and the slider are in step.
  • during the blackout, the solenoid is no longer energised but the slider , depending on the pressure remaining in the main body can (figure 5b) return to the original position or (figure 5e) remain blocked in the new position.
  • after the blackout, if the slider has returned to the original position, it is necessary to wait until the pressure difference grows enough to move (figure 5c) the valve (and the solenoid) into the correct position again ; if the slider has kept the new position, it is necessary to re-energise the solenoid (figure 5f) in order to put it in step with the slider.

Figure 5.

The ReverseValve_2 function block checks for the slider position by reading a Boolean variable stored in the NVRAM (DeltaP_In_Status_NVRAM).

This variable is TRUE if DeltaP_RV >=DeltaPThrsh_RV meaning that the slider has returned to the normal position, or FALSE when DeltaP_RV <DeltaPThrsh_RV-33% meaning that the slider has maintained the position held before the blackout. The value of 33% is chosen according to technical considerations based on the characteristics of the valve.

Figure 6.

In the first case the Function Block has to wait until the actual pressure difference is enough (DeltaP_RV >=DeltaP_Thrsh_RV) before energising the solenoid again and bringing both solenoid and slider into the right position.

In the second case the Function Block must realign the solenoid and the slider by energising the solenoid.

To perform blackout management, DeltaP_In_Status_NVRAM_RV and DoutVlvStatus_NVRAM_RV need to be stored in NVRAM, please refer to Demo_ReverseValve_2 available on the KSA website.
